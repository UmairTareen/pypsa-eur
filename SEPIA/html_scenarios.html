The Negawatt Belgium Scenario

As Belgium faces the challenge of transitioning to a sustainable energy future, understanding different possible pathways becomes increasingly important. This study presents two contrasting scenarios for Belgium's energy transition towards 2050: a Reference scenario and an innovative Sufficiency scenario. Both scenarios aim to achieve a 95% reduction in emissions for Belgium while ensuring net-zero emissions across the broader regional context. These scenarios were developed using the comprehensive PyPSA-Eur model, which provides detailed modeling of electricity and gas networks, industrial demands, and cross-border energy exchanges. The analysis takes into account various factors including renewable energy potential, energy storage capabilities, and the evolution of different sectors such as industry, buildings, and transport.

Sufficiency Scenario

The Sufficiency scenario represents a transformative approach that prioritizes reduced energy consumption and sustainable practices. Key features include:

  • 54% reduction in final energy consumption compared to 2015
  • Higher building renovation rate (3% per year)
  • Increased focus on electrification and renewable energy
  • No reliance on Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)
  • 25% district heating deployment
  • 33% lower total system costs compared to Reference
Reference Scenario

The Reference scenario represents a more conventional approach to energy transition. Key features include:

  • Maintains 2020 demand levels
  • Lower renovation rate (1% per year)
  • Relies on Carbon Capture and Storage technologies
  • Limited district heating deployment
  • Higher proportion of primary steel production (60%)
  • Greater reliance on Direct Air Capture for emissions reduction

Both scenarios demonstrate that a transition to a renewable energy system is feasible for Belgium, with the Sufficiency scenario offering additional benefits in terms of cost reduction, energy independence, and environmental impact.
